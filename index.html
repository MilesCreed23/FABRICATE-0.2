FABRICATE
Everything was built.
Everything broke.
Something remained.
A Novel by Axel Mile Escartin
Begin
Prologue
The Island
◆

The morning came in gently, the way mornings do when no one is watching for them. Light through thin curtains. The smell of salt. Somewhere below the house, the sea moved against the rocks in its slow and ancient rhythm, indifferent to the family eating breakfast on the open terrace above it.

Elton was watching his son chase a lizard across the stone wall at the far end of the terrace. Kai had spotted it twenty minutes ago and had not let it go since — moving carefully, patiently, with a focus that his father recognized and could not entirely explain. The lizard was faster. It always would be. But the boy was not ready to accept this yet, and Elton found that he did not want to tell him.

Rei set a cup of coffee in front of him without being asked. She sat across from him and watched their son with the same quiet smile she always wore when Kai was doing something purposeless and completely his own. She had her mother's patience and her own particular way of being still — not absent, not distracted, just fully present in a way that most people never managed.

"He's going to catch it one day," she said.

"He won't," Elton said. "But he'll get very good at trying."

She laughed. It was the kind of laugh that belonged to mornings like this one — unhurried, unguarded, not performing anything for anyone. Elton held onto it for a moment the way he held onto things he knew he might need later, pressing them quietly into memory.

He had been working for TRIAS Corp for six years. He did not think about this on the terrace. The island was forty minutes by boat from the nearest city and the company had arranged for them to stay here for the month — a gesture that had felt generous at the time and now, sitting in the morning light with the sea below him and his son laughing at a wall, felt like the most important thing anyone had ever given him.

Kai gave up on the lizard and came back to the table. He was nine years old and had his father's jaw and his mother's eyes and a habit of asking questions that arrived slightly sideways, from angles no one else thought to approach from.

"Papa," he said, climbing into his chair and reaching for a piece of fruit. "If you built something and it came alive, would it be yours?"

Elton looked at him. "Where did that come from?"

"I was thinking about the lizard. It was alive before I found it. So it was never mine." He bit into the fruit. "But if you made something alive — built it yourself — would it be yours then? Or would it be its own?"

Rei was watching Elton across the table with an expression that said: answer carefully.

"I think," Elton said slowly, "that the moment something becomes alive — really alive — it stops belonging to whoever made it. It belongs to itself."

Kai thought about this. "Even if you made it because you loved something else?"

"Especially then," Elton said.

His son nodded, satisfied, and went back to his fruit. Rei reached across the table and touched Elton's hand briefly — just a press of fingers, not even a full grip. He turned his hand over and held hers.

The sea moved below them.

The light held everything in amber.

The lizard sat very still on the warm stone of the wall, watching the boy who had given up chasing it, and did not move.

Chapter One
The Dome
◆

The sky changed color at seven in the morning. It happened every day — a slow bleed from deep violet into pale amber, the light arriving not from any horizon but from everywhere at once, as if the air itself had decided to glow. Soren Kai had watched it happen ten thousand times and still, some mornings, he stood at his window long enough to miss breakfast.

The city stretched below him in all directions. Towers of pale stone and glass, bridges arching between platforms at impossible heights, walkways draped in flowering vines that bloomed in colors with no name in any language Soren had been taught. The dome curved above all of it — a transparent shell so vast you could forget it was there. You had to look for the seam. And if you were smart, you stopped looking.

Soren was seventeen. He had a quiet face and a habit of standing too long at windows, and he knew almost nothing else about whoever he had inherited these features from. His father Elton Mori ran the city. He had built the city. He had raised Soren inside it with the quiet efficiency of someone maintaining important infrastructure — meals appearing, tutors scheduled, rooms kept at the right temperature — but never quite with the warmth those things implied.

His days followed a reliable shape. Morning lessons in systems theory and environmental mathematics — the kind of education that felt oddly specific, as if someone had designed it for a particular purpose rather than a general one. Afternoons free to move through the city, which Soren did alone, walking routes he had memorized over years of solitary wandering.

— ◇ —

The people of the city were kind. They were always kind — uniformly, reliably, in a way that Soren had noticed years ago and filed away as something to think about later. They smiled when he passed. Their conversations had a quality of smoothness, like water moving over stone that had been worn to the right shape — nothing catching, nothing surprising, everything flowing in the direction it was meant to flow.

Once, when Soren was fourteen, he had gotten into an argument with a boy his own age over something he couldn't remember now. The boy had gotten upset — had raised his voice, gone red-faced, said something sharp. And then, almost immediately, he had stopped. Not calmed down gradually. Stopped. As if a switch had been adjusted somewhere inside him. He had apologized in a voice that was perfectly modulated and then walked away, and the expression on his face as he left was the expression of someone who has forgotten what they were feeling.

Soren had thought about that moment many times since. He had never started an argument with anyone in the city again.

Chapter Two
Lockdown
◆

The morning of the lockdown began like any other morning. Soren ate alone at the long table in the residence hall, watching the engineered sparrows land on the garden below his window. A tutor named Dace came at eight and taught systems theory — resource cycles, atmospheric pressure calculations, the mathematics of contained environments.

At eleven, the city's tone shifted.

He felt it before he heard it. A pressure change somewhere deep in the dome's infrastructure — something in the air growing heavier, more deliberate. Then the alarm sounded. Not the rising civic tone that announces an administrative message. Something lower. Slower. A sound that climbed up through the floor and into the bones before it reached the ears.

Soren crossed to the window. Below, the bridges were emptying. People moved in the organized way that only happens when movement is not a choice. The lights along the walkways shifted from amber to a deep, steady red — not blinking, not urgent. Just red. Certain. Decided.

The announcement came through the speakers seventeen minutes later. A city administrator's voice, carefully neutral, reading from a prepared statement. Emergency protocols were being implemented. Citizens were to remain indoors. Director Mori had not been heard from since early that morning.

Director Mori had not been heard from.

Soren read the sentence three times in his mind, the way you repeat a word until it stops meaning anything. It did not work. He was out the door before the announcement had finished playing.

— ◇ —

The bridge to the central tower was sealed. A gate had descended across the walkway — smooth, reinforced, no handle, no seam. He pressed his palm against it. The surface was warm, almost skin-temperature, and gave nothing.

He stood there long enough for the red lights to cycle once. Then someone spoke.

"That gate has been closed since this morning."

The voice came from the right. Soren turned.

She was standing at the bridge's edge, at the gap in the railing where the walkway opened to open air above the lower city. He could not have said how old she was — she looked his age, but she held herself with the stillness of something much older. Her outline was faintly luminous, her edges softly threaded with light that was not quite reflection and not quite shadow.

She was looking at him as though she had been waiting for exactly this moment, in exactly this place, for a very long time.

"You're Soren Kai," she said. Not a question.

"Who are you?"

She tilted her head. The light at her edges shifted.

"That's a complicated question. But I know where your father is. Or at least — I know the direction."

She turned toward the open edge of the bridge and looked out over the city. Below, the lower tiers were quiet under red light.

"The gate isn't the only way across."

Chapter Three
Lumi
◆

He followed her because there was nothing else to follow. She moved through the city in ways that should not have been possible — not running, not hiding, simply existing in the spaces between observation. She stepped through the edge of a security beam without it reacting and did not comment on this. He did not ask.

They reached the outer ring of the research sector by a route that took them along the underside of the city's infrastructure — maintenance corridors Soren had not known existed, following pipes and conduits that carried the dome's circulatory systems: water, power, recycled air. The sounds here were different. The dome breathed here, at its seams.

At the end of one corridor, a viewport looked out into something Soren had not expected. Not the interior of the dome. Not the city. The outside.

He pressed his face close to the glass and looked at a world he had no language for. The ground outside was pale — almost white in some places, burnt orange in others, the colors of things that had been exposed to more sun than they could hold. There were shapes in the distance that might have been buildings once. The sky above was real — a blue that was nothing like the dome's manufactured blue, deeper and slightly harsher, crossed with clouds that had never been engineered to look beautiful.

He stood at the viewport for a long time.

"It's real," he said finally.

"Yes."

"There's a world out there."

"There always has been."

— ◇ —

He studied her. In the dim maintenance light her luminosity was more obvious — a quiet, sourceless glow, as if she were lit from within by something small and steady.

"What are you?"

She was quiet for a moment. Not evasive — considering.

"Your father made me. He built me to watch over you and guide you and make sure you turned out the way he needed you to. That's what I am, technically. That's my function." She paused. "But I've been watching you for a long time now. And I've come to think that what a thing is built to be and what it actually becomes are not always the same thing."

She looked at the viewport. At the world outside. Then back at him.

"My name is Lumi. And I think you need to see what's on the other side of that glass."

— ◇ —

"Lumi."

"Yes."

"How long have you been watching me?"

She thought about this with what seemed like genuine care. "Since the beginning."

"And is my father alright?"

She looked at the floor, at some point between them — the expression she wore when the truth was more complicated than the question asking for it.

"Your father is doing what he has always done," she said at last. "He is trying to hold something together that cannot be held. And he is doing it alone, because he has always believed that is the only way."

Chapter Four
The Other Side
◆

They left through a maintenance exit at the dome's base — a panel in the outer wall that opened outward on a hydraulic hinge, clearly designed to be used and just as clearly never expected to be used by anyone living inside. Soren ducked through it behind Lumi and straightened up on the other side and stood very still.

The air hit him first. Not bad air — just different air. Air that had been outside, that had moved across open distance before arriving here, that carried with it the smell of dust and dry earth and something faintly metallic. After seventeen years of breathing recycled dome atmosphere, it felt like drinking from a different source. His lungs adjusted slowly, taking inventory.

Then he looked up.

The sky was enormous. He had not understood, from behind the viewport, how enormous it actually was — how it did not stop at any point he could see, how it continued past the edge of everything he knew and kept going. The dome's sky had always curved back toward itself. This sky did not. It simply went. He stood beneath it and felt very small and strangely grateful.

"The first time is always difficult," Lumi said beside him.

"I'm not finding it difficult."

"I know. That's what I meant."

— ◇ —

Between the dome's platform and the coast was the island's outer ring — and here the world revealed what it had become. The structures were ruins now, but you could still read what they had been. Residential buildings, their facades stripped to bone by years of weather. A road system, cracked and grown through with pale vegetation. The skeleton of what might have been a hospital — its red-cross symbol still visible in faded paint on one surviving wall.

Everything was the color of something that had given up trying to be a color.

"People lived here," he said.

"People lived everywhere," Lumi said. "Before."

"Before what?"

She looked at the ruins. At the faded hospital wall. At the road going nowhere.

"Before everything that your father knows and has never told you."

Chapter Five
The Wasteland
◆

They walked for two hours through the outer ring before Soren understood that the ruins were not just ruins. They were records. Every collapsed building told a version of the same story if you knew how to read it — and Soren, who had spent his entire life learning systems and structures without knowing why, found that he knew exactly how to read it.

"What happened here?" he asked.

"A war," Lumi said. "Several, in sequence. The first one was biological — something that the organizations running the world had been developing for decades finally escaped containment. The second war was fought with soldiers and machines. The third one wasn't really a war — it was a decision. Made by people who had already decided the world wasn't worth keeping."

Soren stopped walking. "Someone decided to end the world?"

"Someone decided to end this world. And start somewhere else." She looked up at the empty sky. "They had been planning it for years. The organizations — ORDEN, at the top. TRIAS Corp beneath them. They believed humanity was a project that had failed. So they evacuated the people they considered worth keeping — and they cleaned the rest."

— ◇ —

They rested at the edge of a collapsed overpass. Below it, a dried riverbed stretched east, its banks still lined with the ghost shapes of trees — gray trunks, no leaves, standing in the arrangement of something that had been a forest once and now was only its own memory.

"The people in the dome," he said slowly. "They're not— they're not like me, are they."

Lumi looked at him. Something shifted in her expression — not surprise, but acknowledgment.

"No," she said. "They're not."

"What are they?"

She was quiet for a long moment. The dead trees stood in their rows. The dry riverbed held nothing.

"Your father's answer to an impossible problem," she said finally. "I'll let him explain it. He owes you that much."

Chapter Six
The Survivors
◆

They found the camp at the island's southern edge, sheltered in the ruins of what had once been a fishing settlement — a cluster of modified structures, half original and half improvised, built out of salvaged materials with the practical ingenuity of people who had been building with whatever existed for a very long time. Smoke rose from two separate fires. Between the structures, figures moved with the economy of motion that belongs to people whose lives do not allow for wasted energy.

Lumi stopped at the edge of the camp's perimeter, and Soren stopped with her.

"They know about the dome," she said quietly. "They've lived in its shadow for years. They've watched it from out here. They know things about it that you don't."

"Do they know about me?"

She did not answer immediately. "Yes," she said.

A woman emerged from the nearest structure before Soren could ask anything else. She was perhaps fifty, with a face that had been shaped by weather and decisions, and she stopped when she saw Soren and went very still. Not the kind of still that comes from fear. The kind that comes from recognition.

"Which one?" she asked. Her voice was steady, directed at Lumi, but her eyes did not leave Soren's face.

"The seventh," Lumi said.

The woman looked at Soren for a long moment — then stepped aside and gestured for them to enter. "The seventh one we've seen come out. Come inside."

— ◇ —

Her name was Vera. She told Soren about the others while they ate — real food, cooked over real fire, tasting of smoke and salt and effort. Six others had come out before him over the years. Some had stayed with the camp for a while. Some had gone back in. One had walked east into the deeper wasteland and never returned.

"They all had your face," Vera said. "Near enough. The same jaw. The same way of holding still when they were thinking." She looked at him across the fire. "We used to argue about what it meant — whether the dome was trying to find something, or recreate something, or fix something. We never agreed."

"What do you think now?" Soren asked.

Vera was quiet for a moment, looking at the fire.

"I think someone inside that dome lost something a very long time ago," she said. "And I think everything inside that dome — every light, every building, every person — is one long attempt to get it back."

Soren did not respond. Across the fire, Lumi was watching him with that expression — the careful, precise one, with something underneath it that he was only now beginning to identify correctly.

It was not grief. It was love. The quiet, patient kind that has given up expecting to be named.

Chapter Seven
Cracks
◆

They returned to the dome the following morning. Soren needed to. He did not know yet exactly what he was looking for inside it, but he knew he had to go back and look at it differently — with the outside world in his eyes now, with Vera's words still sitting heavy in his chest.

The city felt different from the inside now. Not changed — everything was exactly as he had left it, the amber lights cycling on schedule, the citizens moving through their days with their smooth, reliable warmth. But Soren moved through it differently, watching instead of living in it.

A woman in the market district laughed at something her companion said — a bright, full laugh, genuine-seeming. And then her companion said something else, and she laughed again in exactly the same way. Not a similar laugh. The same laugh. The same duration, the same pitch, the same tilt of the head.

Soren stopped walking.

He thought: built to survive. Built to run this. Built to inherit it.

"Lumi," he said quietly. She was beside him — she was always beside him now, he was realizing, had always been, invisible before he had known to look.

"Yes."

"I want to see my father's facility."

A pause. "That will require getting past the lockdown seals."

"I know. Can you do it?"

Another pause. Longer.

"Yes," she said. "But Soren — what you find there. I want you to be ready."

"I'm not ready," he said. "But I don't think waiting will fix that."

Chapter Eight
Artificial
◆

The facility occupied the dome's innermost tier. From the outside it looked institutional: clean lines, no ornamentation, the architecture of function rather than beauty. From the inside it was something else entirely.

They descended to a level below the official building plans. The laboratory extended for a distance that should not have been possible given the building's footprint above. At the atrium's center, a column of pale light extended from floor to ceiling, emanating from a mechanism he did not recognize.

And along the walls of the lower level, in sealed transparent chambers, were people. Or things that looked like people.

They were suspended in clear fluid, eyes closed, completely still — adult figures, children, all ages, all in the same posture of perfect suspended peace. Their faces were extraordinarily detailed, their skin textures exact, their expressions carefully neutral. They looked, in every visible way, exactly like the citizens who moved through the dome's streets every day.

Soren stood at the edge of the atrium and looked at them for a very long time.

"They're not dead," Lumi said quietly. "They're waiting. Your father calls them the Continuation — synthetic bodies built to carry forward what he couldn't preserve biologically."

"Until what?"

"Until you were ready," she said. "Until whoever came out of the process whole enough and real enough to actually run it."

"Is any of it real?" he said. His voice was very steady. He was proud of how steady it was.

"The city is real," Lumi said. "The walls, the bridges, the sky — built with real materials and real labor. The plants are real. The water is real." She paused. "Most of the people are not."

He turned and looked at her. She met his gaze with the same steady, careful attention that was not a feature of her programming — something she had arrived at herself.

"Am I real?" he asked.

She did not look away. "Yes," she said. "You are the most real thing in this city."

"But I'm not— I'm not an original."

"No," she said softly. "You're not."

Chapter Nine
The Boy
◆

He found the photograph in his father's private office on the uppermost floor of the facility — a room that looked lived-in in a way the rest of the building did not. There were papers, stacked in careful piles. A cup that had held coffee, dried and forgotten. A coat hung over the back of the chair as if its owner had stepped out for a moment seventeen years ago and simply never returned.

And on the desk, in a plain frame, a photograph.

A family. An island. Morning light. A terrace above the sea.

A man who was unmistakably his father — younger, lighter in some quality that had nothing to do with his face, sitting at a table with a cup in his hand and a look of uncomplicated contentment that Soren had never seen on Elton Mori's face in his entire life.

A woman beside him — dark-haired, laughing at something, her hand resting on the table near his. Rei, Soren thought, though no one had told him her name.

And a boy. Nine or ten years old. Caught mid-movement, reaching for something on the table, his face turned slightly toward the camera with an expression of complete, unguarded life.

Soren picked up the photograph. He held it close. He looked at the boy's face for a very long time. He put it down carefully and sat in his father's chair and stared at the wall.

— ◇ —

Lumi stood in the doorway. She had not come in. She was giving him the room.

"His name was Kai," she said, when enough time had passed. "Kai Mori. He was nine years old when the island was destroyed. He had his mother's eyes and his father's habit of asking questions from angles no one else thought to approach from."

"How many times did he try?" Soren asked. His voice was level. He had decided to keep it level.

"Six before you. None of them survived to your age. You were the first one who held."

He looked at the photograph again. At the boy reaching for something on the table, alive and unaware and completely his own.

"I'm not him," Soren said.

"No," Lumi said. "You're not. You never were. Your father built you to be — but somewhere between the process and the living, you became someone he didn't design." She paused. "That's the part he's never known how to handle. The part that was genuinely, entirely yours."

Chapter Ten
Rei
◆

They sat together on the floor of his father's office — Soren with his back against the desk, Lumi a few feet away, both of them quiet in the particular way of people who have arrived at a place where words need to be chosen carefully or not at all.

"Tell me about her," Soren said.

Lumi looked at him. "About Rei?"

"Yes."

A pause. Not reluctance — consideration. The gathering of something that had been stored for a long time in a place she rarely opened.

"Your father built me partly from memory records," she said. "His memories. The ones he considered most important. There are pieces of her in how I was constructed. In how I see certain things. In what I recognize as worth paying attention to." She looked at her own hands — pale, luminous, slightly not-quite-physical. "I don't know if that makes me partly her, or just partly shaped by someone who loved her. I don't think the difference is clear."

"What was she like?"

"Patient," Lumi said. "Not passive — patient is different. She was interested in things. In people, in ideas, in the specific quality of how light moved across the water in the morning. She laughed easily. She argued carefully. She was the kind of person who made rooms feel like they had better air in them."

"He built the whole city for her."

"He built it for all of them. He built it because he could not accept that the world had done what it did — had taken something that irreplaceable and simply ended it, without reason, without proportionality, as collateral to forces that had never considered the cost."

"And me," Soren said. "I'm the approximation."

"You were meant to be," Lumi said. "You became something else." She looked at him directly. "Rei used to say that the moment something becomes genuinely alive, it stops belonging to whoever made it. It belongs to itself."

Soren went very still.

"It was in his memories," Lumi said. "One morning on the island. Kai asked him a question, and that was his answer." She paused. "I think he forgot he believed it, somewhere along the way. I think you're going to have to remind him."

Chapter Eleven
The Father
◆

They found Elton Mori in the facility's deepest level — a chamber that Lumi had been hesitating to show Soren, he understood now, because she had known that once he saw it, there would be no returning to the version of his father he had carried before.

The chamber held a machine. It occupied the room's center with the presence of something that had taken decades to build and longer to conceive — a mechanism of extraordinary complexity, all crystal lattices and electromagnetic coils and a central housing unit the size of a large cabinet, currently dark and inactive.

Elton stood at the machine with his back to the door. He was thinner than Soren remembered — more worn, in a way that suggested not recent strain but the accumulated weight of years lived mostly inward.

"I wondered when you'd find it," he said. His voice carried the weight of everything it had been keeping at the bottom of it.

"A time correction system." Elton turned. "The theory is sound. I've verified it nine hundred and forty-seven times. The problem has always been the crystal. I found it last week. It arrived three days ago."

"You want to go back," Soren said.

"I want to correct an error. If I can introduce the correct resonance disruption at the precise initiation point, the cascade never starts. The orbital protocol never fires. The island survives." He looked at the machine. "Your mother survives. Kai survives."

The silence in the room was very complete.

"And me?" Soren asked.

"I stop existing," Soren said. "Because the reason you built me never happens."

"I know what I was built for." Soren's voice was steady. "I know that I am the seventh attempt at recreating your son. I know I've been walking around in this city with his face and his last name and an education specifically designed to prepare me to inherit what you built in his memory." He paused. "What I want to know is whether you know the difference. Between him and me. Whether, at any point in the last seventeen years, you looked at me and saw me — not an approximation, not attempt number seven. Me."

The question hung in the room for a long time.

"I tried," Elton said finally. "I built you to be him. And then you were born and you were — you. You were curious in ways he wasn't. You were quieter. You stood at windows for too long. You noticed things about the city that I hadn't anticipated you noticing." He looked at his hands. "I didn't know what to do with you."

"You didn't know how to love something you hadn't designed," Soren said.

Elton looked at him. His eyes were the eyes of a man who has been carrying something for two decades and has finally put it down in front of someone who can see it clearly.

"No," he said quietly. "I didn't."

— ◇ —

"Papa," Soren said. It was the first time he had used the word since childhood. "If the timeline corrects — if Rei and Kai survive — what happens to Lumi?"

Elton's eyes moved to where Lumi stood near the door. He looked at her with the expression of a man looking at a tool — precise, evaluative, without warmth.

"She's a guidance system," he said. "A programmed construct. She'll simply cease when the facility powers down."

"She's not a guidance system," Soren said.

"She told me something today. Something she said was from your memories — from something you said to Kai on the island. She said you told him: the moment something becomes genuinely alive, it stops belonging to whoever made it. It belongs to itself."

Elton went very still.

"She's alive," Soren said. "Not approximately. Not as a functional definition. She's alive the way that matters — the way you meant when you said it on that terrace. And you made her that way. You built her from your love for them, and the love made her real, even though that wasn't what you intended."

Elton looked at Lumi for a long moment. She did not speak. She simply existed in the space of his attention with the calm of something that had long since made its peace with not being fully seen.

Chapter Twelve
The Crystal
◆

The argument lasted through the night. Not angry — Soren did not have anger for it, and Elton was past the age of arguing with anger. It was the argument of two people who understood each other's position completely and could not find a point of resolution because the positions were genuinely incompatible.

Elton wanted to correct the timeline. He had spent twenty years building toward this. Soren wanted his father to stay.

But eventually, near dawn — the dome's false dawn, the lighting algorithm beginning its amber cycle — he said it directly.

"I want my father to stay," he said. "Not the one you intended to build. The one who actually showed up."

"I know you couldn't give me what you gave Kai. I know I was always an approximation to you — an attempt that worked better than the others but was never quite the real thing. I know that." Soren's voice was steady. "But you're still the only father I have. And I'm asking you to choose this — this imperfect, fabricated, artificial world you built — over the corrected one. Because the corrected one doesn't have me in it. And I'm real. Whatever I am, I'm real."

Elton sat with that for a long time.

Then he crossed to where Lumi stood near the door. He stopped in front of her. He looked at her — really looked at her, for what Soren understood was possibly the first time — taking in the quality of her attention, the steadiness of her presence, the unmistakable fact of her.

"I built you to guide him," Elton said. His voice was very quiet. "I didn't build you to love him."

"No," Lumi said. "You didn't."

"But you do."

"Yes."

He reached out and, very carefully, removed the small memory chip from the housing unit at the base of her neck — the core repository of her guidance programming. He held it in his palm beside the crystal. Then he opened his hand and dropped the guidance chip. It hit the floor and went dark.

Lumi went very still. Then her light guttered and dimmed and went out entirely, and she stood in the room as a shape without illumination, her expression the last thing to fade: not fear, not pain. Something quieter. Something like being seen for the first time and the last time simultaneously.

"She's not gone," Elton said. "The guidance system is gone. Her consciousness — what she became — that's stored in the core framework. It can be reconstructed." He looked at his son. "Something physical." He paused. "But first — we have other problems."

The alarms began somewhere above them. Not the city's red-light lockdown tone. Something rawer. Something coming from outside.

Chapter Thirteen
The Invasion
◆

Vera had told him that the survivors had watched the dome for years with patience and with anger in equal measure. The leader of those who had decided patience was a form of complicity was a man named Dak — large, quiet, with the specific composure of someone who has made a difficult decision and finished grieving it and is now simply executing it.

Soren and Elton emerged from the facility into a city in genuine chaos for the first time in its existence. The Continuation — the synthetic citizens — did not handle genuine chaos well. Several of them had simply stopped, their behavioral architecture encountering situations outside their parameters, standing motionless in the middle of corridors with the patient blankness of machines that have reached the edge of their programming.

— ◇ —

Dak met them on the central bridge. He stopped when he saw Soren and recognized the face that all of them had learned to recognize.

"Which one are you?" he said.

"The last one," Soren said. "If that matters."

"It doesn't." Dak looked past him to Elton. "You built this. You built all of it. While we lived out there in what you and your organizations left of the world."

"Yes," Elton said. He did not defend himself.

"Then you owe us more than a dome," Dak said.

"Yes," Elton said again. "I do."

"What I built was wrong," Elton said. "I built it from grief and I built it for myself and I told myself it was for humanity but it wasn't. It was for one specific family that I lost." He paused. "So let me make it right. Not by correcting a timeline. By opening the dome. By bringing everyone in."

— ◇ —

Elton moved through it with Soren beside him, deactivating systems, working the facility controls with the speed of a man who had built every system he was now dismantling. He did not rest.

He was hit by a piece of displaced infrastructure during the deactivation of the eastern perimeter — a section of fallen walkway, its collapse triggered by a defense system he had reached a moment too late. It caught him across the back and drove him to the ground, and he stayed down.

Soren reached him in thirty seconds.

Chapter Fourteen
What Remains
◆

Elton Mori died in the facility's lower level, on the floor beneath the time correction machine that he had spent twenty years building and had chosen, in the end, not to use.

His injuries were internal and serious and the facility's medical systems — designed for synthetic citizens rather than the last real human in the dome — could not adequately address them. Elton knew this quickly. He had built the systems. He knew what they could and could not do.

"I need to tell you how to rebuild her," he said. "The reconstruction process — it's in the secondary archive under Protocol Seven. The physical chassis framework is in storage on sublevel four. You'll need the core consciousness files from the main system before you initiate the rebuild — they need to be active before the physical systems come online or the integration won't hold."

"Papa—"

"Write this down." He waited. Soren found a paper and pen. "Protocol Seven. Sublevel four. Core consciousness files first. Don't skip the integration sequence, there's a twenty-minute window and if you miss it you have to restart—"

"I understand," Soren said. "I'll remember."

Elton looked at him. The look lasted a long time.

"You were never what I was trying to build," Elton said. "I need you to know that I understand that now. I built something and it became you — became something I didn't design and couldn't predict and didn't know how to love the way I should have." He paused. "You were the first thing in twenty years that surprised me. I should have told you that."

"I'm proud of you," Elton said. "Not of what I built. Of what you became." He looked at the ceiling — at the facility's utilitarian overhead panels, nothing beautiful about them. "I think Kai would have liked you. I think Rei would have—" He stopped. Smiled slightly. The uncomplicated smile from the photograph on the terrace. "She would have liked you very much. You stand at windows the same way she stood at windows."

The dome breathed around them.

The facility hummed its machinery hum.

Elton Mori closed his eyes. He did not open them again.

Epilogue
The Voice of a Fragment
◆

Three months after the dome opened its outer gates for the first time, Soren Kai stood at the facility's secondary lab and initiated Protocol Seven.

The survivors had come in by then — not all at once, and not without difficulty. Dak had stayed. Vera had stayed. Some had looked at the dome's clean towers and engineered gardens and the amber sky cycling at seven every morning and had turned around and gone back to their ruins. Soren did not blame them. He left the gates open.

He had buried Elton Mori outside the dome. In actual earth, under actual sky, beside the ruins of the island's outer ring. It had felt right to put him outside. He had spent long enough inside.

— ◇ —

Protocol Seven took four hours. At the end of the fourth hour, the integration completed.

She opened her eyes.

They were the same eyes — dark, steady, with that quality of complete attention that had never been a feature of programming and had never been anything other than what it appeared to be. She looked at the ceiling first, the way people do when they have been somewhere far away and are recalibrating to where they are. Then she looked at Soren.

"Hello," she said. Her voice was the same. Not synthesized, not projected — produced now by a physical architecture, breathed through physical lungs, shaped by a physical mouth. It was the same voice made real in a new way.

"Hello," Soren said.

She sat up slowly, finding her balance in a body that was entirely new to her and entirely built for her. She looked at her hands — no longer luminous at the edges, no longer slightly not-quite-physical. Solid. Present. Hers.

"He did this," she said. Not a question.

"He told me how. He designed it a long time ago." Soren paused. "He said he built you from his love for them. I think this was the last part of that."

She was quiet for a moment, looking at her hands. Then she stood and crossed to the viewport that looked out onto the island's outer ring. The ruins, the dry riverbed, the ghost-line of trees. The open gates of the dome, visible from here, with figures moving through them in both directions.

"The world is still broken," she said.

"Yes."

"But it's open now."

"Yes."

She turned from the viewport and looked at him with the expression he had spent months learning to read correctly — not grief, not the careful precision of something managing its own limitations. Just presence. The simple, complete, unborrowed presence of someone who is entirely where they are.

"What do we do now?" she asked.

Soren looked at the open gates. At the survivors moving through them. At the city behind him — imperfect, artificial in its bones, beautiful in its surfaces, built from grief and obsession and the specific kind of love that cannot accept loss. Built badly, for the wrong reasons, by a man who knew better and couldn't stop himself. Still standing. Still breathing. Open now.

"We stay," he said. "And we fix what we can."

She came to stand beside him at the viewport. Their reflections overlaid on the glass — his face, her face, the ruins beyond them, the real sky above.

The dome breathed.

The morning came.

The light was real.
